How Position Sizing Works

01

Define Your Risk

Decide what percentage of your account you are willing to lose on this trade. Most professional traders use 1–2%. At 2%, you need 50 consecutive losses to lose everything.

02

Set Your Stop Loss

Your stop loss defines where you exit if the trade goes against you. The further it is from your entry, the smaller your position must be to stay within your risk budget.

03

The Formula

Size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ |Entry − Stop|
This gives you the exact number of units to open so that hitting your stop costs exactly your risk amount — no more.

Frequently Asked Questions

Position sizing determines how many units of an asset to buy or sell per trade. It is arguably the single most important factor in long-term trading survival. Correct sizing ensures that no single loss can cause catastrophic damage to your account — regardless of the asset's volatility or your win rate.

The most widely recommended range is 1–2% per trade. Beginners should start at 0.5–1%. Some high-conviction traders go up to 3–5%, but this materially increases peak drawdown. Risking more than 5% per trade is generally considered reckless for any account size.

A minimum R:R of 1.5 : 1 is generally acceptable; 2 : 1 or higher is preferred. At 2:1, you only need to win 33% of your trades to break even. Avoid trades below 1:1 — even a high win rate cannot compensate for poor reward relative to risk in the long run.

The estimate uses a simplified isolated-margin formula with a 0.5% maintenance margin rate:

  • Long: Entry × (1 − 1/Leverage + 0.005)
  • Short: Entry × (1 + 1/Leverage − 0.005)

Actual liquidation prices vary by exchange, leverage tier, and whether you use isolated or cross margin. Always confirm the exact price on your exchange before opening a leveraged position.
